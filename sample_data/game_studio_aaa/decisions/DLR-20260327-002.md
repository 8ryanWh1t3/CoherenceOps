# DLR-20260327-002: Global Incident Response SOP

## Context

The W09-W12 crisis cycle exposed the absence of a formal cross-studio incident response procedure. During the tournament CCU spike (W10), the monetization backlash (W11), and the regulatory pressure (W12), each studio improvised its own response: LA used a Slack war room, Montreal used Zoom bridges, Tokyo used an internal wiki-based runbook, and Berlin/Warsaw had no documented process. The Emergency Hotfix Bypass Protocol (DLR-20260306-002) and Cross-Studio Hotfix Cadence Alignment (DLR-20260313-003) addressed specific symptoms but not the systemic lack of coordination. EVID-0003 (EU Regulatory Notice Scan and Legal Memo) documented that the regulatory response took 14h to assemble cross-studio because no one knew who owned what. Post-mortem analysis identified 7 separate instances where response time could have been cut by 40-60% with a standardized SOP. OrionTel and VantaFuel sponsor contracts both include "reasonable response time" clauses that were strained during W10-W11.

## Options Considered

1. **Formal cross-studio SOP with defined roles and automation (selected)** -- documented Standard Operating Procedure covering sev-1 through sev-3 incidents. Defined Incident Commander rotation, communication channels, escalation paths, and post-incident review process. PagerDuty automation for alerting and war room creation.
2. **LA-centric incident command** -- all incidents flow through LA regardless of origin. Simpler but creates timezone bottleneck and single point of failure.
3. **Studio-autonomous response with post-incident sync** -- each studio handles incidents independently and syncs afterward. Fastest initial response but no coordination during multi-studio incidents.
4. **Outsource to managed incident response vendor** -- contract PagerDuty Professional Services or FireHydrant managed response. Fast implementation but external dependency for game-specific triage.

## Decision

Adopt Option 1. Publish a Global Incident Response SOP governing all sev-1 through sev-3 incidents across all five studios. Key elements:

- **Incident Commander (IC)**: Rotating weekly role across LA, Montreal, Tokyo. IC has authority to mobilize any studio resource during sev-1.
- **Communication**: Automated PagerDuty → Slack war room creation within 2 minutes of sev-1 declaration. Zoom bridge auto-spawns for sev-1 and sev-2.
- **Escalation**: Sev-3 (studio-local, 4h response), Sev-2 (multi-studio, 1h response), Sev-1 (all-hands, 15-min response with exec notification).
- **Post-Incident**: Blameless post-mortem within 48h. Action items tracked in Jira with owner and due date. Post-mortem published to all studios.
- **Sponsor notification**: OrionTel and VantaFuel contacts notified within 30 min of any sev-1 that may impact uptime SLA.

## Trade-offs

- (+) Eliminates ad-hoc response; every studio knows their role before an incident occurs.
- (+) IC rotation distributes load and prevents LA burnout.
- (+) Automated tooling (PagerDuty + Slack + Zoom) removes manual setup overhead during crisis.
- (+) Sponsor notification protocol protects $30M/yr in combined contract value.
- (-) SOP documentation and training requires ~200 person-hours across 5 studios.
- (-) IC rotation requires on-call commitment from senior engineers in all timezones.
- (-) Over-process risk: sev-3 incidents may not warrant full SOP activation.

## Assumptions

- **DLR-20260306-002**: Emergency hotfix bypass protocol integrates into SOP as the sev-1 patch fast-track.
- **DLR-20260313-003**: Cross-studio hotfix cadence alignment defines the patch flow that SOP coordinates.
- **EVID-0003**: EU regulatory response timeline used as baseline for "worst case" response scenario in SOP drills.

## Kill Switch / Rollback

SOP is a process document, not a technical system. Rollback means reverting to pre-SOP ad-hoc response. PagerDuty automation can be disabled per-studio. IC rotation paused by removing on-call schedule. Rollback time: immediate (process revert; no technical dependencies).
